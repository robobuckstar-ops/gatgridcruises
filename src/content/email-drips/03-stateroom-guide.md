## Body

Hi {{contact.FIRSTNAME | default: "there"}},

Disney has 11 stateroom categories. They're numbered. Lower = nicer.

That sounds simple until you're staring at a booking screen with categories
4A, 4B, 4C, 4D, 4E, 5A, 5B… and wondering whether the difference is real or
made up.

Here's how I'd think about it for a typical family of four:

### The "default" answer: a Verandah (categories 4–7)

If your budget allows, get the verandah. Almost every family we work with who
got one says they'd never go back. Three reasons:

1. **Natural light all day** — the cabin doesn't feel like a hotel room
2. **Quiet morning coffee** while the kids sleep
3. **Sea days transform** — you actually use the cabin, instead of escaping it

The price gap between a verandah and an oceanview is real (often $400–$800
per person on a 7-night), but it's usually the upgrade that delivers.

### The big secret: split bathrooms

Most Disney cabins (categories 1–9 on the Dream-class and Wish-class ships,
many on the Magic and Wonder) include a **split bath** — one room with sink
and toilet, another with sink and shower/tub.

With four people getting ready for dinner, this single layout decision saves
your sanity. Ask your advisor (or the booking page) to confirm split bath is
in your specific room. It's not in every category.

### Budget category? Pick an "Oceanview" (8–9)

A porthole or large window instead of a balcony. Same cabin footprint, same
split bathroom on most ships. ~25–35% less than verandahs. Best move for
families who plan to spend almost zero time in-cabin.

### Skip the Inside (10–11) unless…

You're a couple with no kids, you'll be off-ship every port day, and you
genuinely don't need natural light. Otherwise, the savings don't outweigh
the cabin-fever cost on a 7-night sailing.

(Note: Dream-class ships offer **"Magical Portholes"** in inside cabins — a
high-def screen that simulates a view. They're a fun feature but not a
substitute for a real window.)

### "Guarantee" (GTY) — worth it?

When you book GTY, you commit to a category (or better), but Disney assigns
the specific room. You usually save a couple hundred dollars and sometimes
get upgraded. Risks: you can't pick your deck, you can't avoid the noisy
locations (under pool decks, over showrooms), and you can't book accessible
features.

**Good for**: flexible cruisers, second-time sailings, short trips.
**Skip if**: you're traveling with someone motion-sensitive, you need an
accessible cabin, or you're particular about location.

---

**One more rule of thumb:** mid-ship, mid-deck cabins minimize motion. Aft
verandahs are larger on some ships. Avoid Deck 2 under the galley — early-
morning prep noise carries. Ask your advisor to pull the deck plan for your
specific category before you book.
